\begin{abstract}
We examine how a 2018 disclosure-regime reform in [Country X] affected cross-border
capital allocation. Using a difference-in-differences design with synthetic-control
comparison units, we find that disclosure transparency increases foreign portfolio
investment by roughly 22\% over a three-year horizon, with the strongest effects
concentrated in mid-cap firms. Our estimates are robust to alternative event windows,
control-group selection, and exclusion of financial firms. We interpret these results
through the lens of \citet{Merton1987} and discuss implications for emerging-market
disclosure standards.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The relationship between corporate-information disclosure and cross-border capital
flows has been a long-standing question in international finance. Theoretical work
\citep{Merton1987, Easley2004} predicts that information asymmetry creates barriers
to entry for non-domestic investors. Empirical work using cross-country variation
has been suggestive but limited by confounding policy reforms.

In this paper, we exploit a sharp regulatory reform in [Country X] in 2018 that
mandated quarterly disclosure of beneficial-ownership structure for all listed firms.
The reform was announced in 2017 and took effect on January 1, 2018, providing a
clean before-after design.

\subsection{Sample Construction}

Our analysis sample consists of [Country X]-listed firms in 2014--2022. We exclude
financial firms (SIC 6000-6999) and firms with fewer than 8 quarters of pre-period data,
yielding a final sample of 14,562 firm-year observations.

\subsection{Identification}

We use a difference-in-differences design comparing affected firms to a synthetic-control
unit constructed from neighboring [Region] markets without disclosure reforms in 2018.
The identifying assumption is that, absent the reform, treated and synthetic-control
firms would have followed parallel trends in foreign portfolio investment.

\section{Conclusion}

We provide quasi-experimental evidence that disclosure-regime reforms increase
cross-border portfolio flows. The implications for emerging-market regulators are
direct: even modest, well-targeted disclosure mandates can substantially shift
the cost of capital for affected firms.
